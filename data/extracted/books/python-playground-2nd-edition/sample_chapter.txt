                Using Recursion
                To get a feel for how recursion works, letÔÇÖs take a look at a simple recursive
                algorithm: computing the factorial of a number. The factorial of a number
                can be defined by a function, as shown here:

                                         f(N) = 1 ├ù 2 ├ù 3 ├ù . . . ├ù (N ÔêÆ 1) ├ù N

                    In other words, the factorial of N is just the product of the numbers 1
                through N. You can rewrite this as:

                                         f(N) = N ├ù (N ÔêÆ┬¡1) ├ù . . . ├ù 3 ├ù 2 ├ù 1

                which can again be rewritten as:

                                                  f(N) = N ├ù f(N ÔêÆ 1)

                    Wait, what did you just do? You defined f in terms of itself! ThatÔÇÖs recur-
                sion. Calling f(N ) will end up calling f(N ÔêÆ┬¡1), which will end up calling
                f(N ÔêÆ┬¡2), and so on. But how do you know when to stop? Well, you have to
                define f(1) as 1, and that will be the deepest step of the recursion.

perfect example of this partitioning, and as youÔÇÖll soon see, so is drawing
the Koch snowflake. That said, recursion isnÔÇÖt always the most efficient way
to solve a problem. In some cases, it would make sense to re-┬¡implement the
recursive algorithm in terms of loops. But the fact remains that recursive
algorithms are often more compact and elegant compared to their loopy
counterparts.
